It is often claimed that printed books and newspapers will eventually become obsolete, as people will be able to access all reading materials online free of charge. However, I strongly disagree with this view, as it overlooks both the economic realities of content creation and the enduring value of printed media.
Admittedly, the Internet has revolutionised access to information, making a vast amount of content readily available. Nevertheless, it is unrealistic to assume that everything can be accessed without cost. A considerable proportion of high-quality material is restricted by paywalls, as creators and publishers seek to monetise their work. Moreover, copyright regulations play a crucial role in protecting intellectual property, thereby limiting the free distribution of content. For instance, many digital platforms now operate on subscription-based models, offering exclusive articles, research, or insights that are only accessible to paying users. This demonstrates that, despite the abundance of free information, a large share of valuable content remains commercialised.
In addition, it is unlikely that printed materials will disappear entirely, even in an increasingly digital world. In academic and professional contexts, such as medicine and law, printed texts are often regarded as more reliable and authoritative sources. Furthermore, physical books offer a tangible reading experience that many individuals continue to value. Book collectors and enthusiasts, in particular, appreciate the aesthetic and sentimental qualities of printed editions, which digital formats cannot replicate. As a result, demand for printed materials, although reduced, is likely to persist.
In conclusion, while digital technology has significantly expanded access to information, it is improbable that all content will become freely available or that printed books and newspapers will vanish altogether. Both economic factors and reader preferences suggest that traditional and digital formats will continue to coexist. (288)
